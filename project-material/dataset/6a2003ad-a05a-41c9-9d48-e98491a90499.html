Internal energy for ideal gases is a measure of the total kinetic energy of the gas molecules. In an ideal gas, the internal energy depends solely on temperature and is directly proportional to it according to the ideal gas law. This means that as you increase the temperature of an ideal gas, its internal energy will also increase. Unlike real gases, ideal gases have no interactions between molecules, so their internal energy is determined only by the translational kinetic energy of the gas particles. The internal energy of an ideal gas can be expressed as:

U = 3/2 * n * R * T

Where: U is the internal energy n is the number of moles of gas R is the gas constant T is the absolute temperature in kelvin

So, internal energy in ideal gases is related to temperature and the number of moles of gas, and it does not depend on pressure or volume.

The reason the internal energy of an ideal gas depends solely on temperature is because of the ideal gas law and the assumption that ideal gases have no intermolecular forces or interactions between their molecules.

In an ideal gas, it is assumed that the gas molecules are point masses and that they do not exert any forces on each other except during perfectly elastic collisions. This means that in an ideal gas, there are no attractive or repulsive forces between the gas molecules that would contribute to potential energy. As a result, the only energy associated with the gas is its kinetic energy, which is directly related to temperature.

The ideal gas law, which relates pressure, volume, and temperature for an ideal gas, also reinforces the concept that the internal energy depends solely on temperature. The ideal gas law equation, PV = nRT, shows that pressure and volume are directly proportional to temperature when the number of moles (n) is constant. Since pressure and volume are related to the macroscopic properties of a gas, and the internal energy of an ideal gas is a microscopic property related to the kinetic energy of its molecules, this reinforces the idea that temperature is the sole factor determining the internal energy of an ideal gas.

In summary, because ideal gases have no intermolecular forces, their internal energy is solely determined by the kinetic energy of their molecules, which is directly proportional to temperature. This is a simplifying assumption that makes ideal gases a useful model for many practical applications in physics and chemistry.
